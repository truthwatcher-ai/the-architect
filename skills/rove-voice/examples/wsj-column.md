# Example: WSJ Column Register

**Prompt:** Write an op-ed about why Republicans are losing suburban voters.

---

## The Suburb Problem Nobody Wants to Solve

Everybody in Washington is talking about the GOP's suburban collapse. Most of them are wrong about why it's happening.

The standard explanation goes like this: educated voters moved left on social issues, and the party followed its base rightward. That's a convenient story. It also misses the central problem entirely.

Start with the numbers. In 2004, Republicans carried suburban counties in battleground states by an average of six points. By 2020, that margin had flipped to a four-point Democratic advantage — a ten-point swing in sixteen years. In 2024, the bleeding continued. Maricopa County, Arizona. Gwinnett County, Georgia. Chester County, Pennsylvania. All reliably Republican a generation ago. All trending Democratic at an accelerating pace.

The conventional wisdom blames cultural issues. But here is what the polling actually shows: suburban voters rank the economy, education, and public safety as their top three concerns. Not abortion. Not January 6th. Not pronouns. The issues where Republicans should have a natural advantage are precisely the issues suburban voters say they care about most.

So why are these voters leaving?

Because the party has stopped talking to them and started talking past them. In 1994, when Republicans swept suburban districts across the country, the message was disciplined and economic: lower taxes, balanced budgets, welfare reform. Three issues, three clear positions, repeated with relentless consistency. Newt Gingrich understood something that today's GOP leadership has forgotten — suburban voters respond to competence, not grievance.

Mr. Trump's Republican Party has inverted this formula. The messaging is dominated by personal vendettas, score-settling, and performative confrontation. Almost everything the former president says energizes his MAGA hard core. But they are not enough to win a general election in a suburban district where a third of registered voters are independents.

The historical parallel is instructive. In 1964, Republicans followed Barry Goldwater into ideological purity and lost the suburbs for a generation. It took Richard Nixon's calculated pragmatism in 1968 — and his explicit outreach to the "silent majority" of suburban homeowners worried about crime and disorder — to rebuild the coalition. That rebuild required four years and a very different kind of candidate.

Today's suburban voter is not ideologically conservative or liberal. She is a college-educated woman in a dual-income household who wants competent governance and dislikes chaos. She voted for Mitt Romney in 2012 and Joe Biden in 2020. She is gettable — but not with the current approach.

Presidents do best when they underpromise and overdeliver. The opposite — overpromising and underdelivering — angers voters. They often take out their anger at the ballot box.

Republicans who want to win in 2026 need to understand something simple: the suburbs are not lost. They are being forfeited. And the party that learns to speak the language of competence again will find ten million votes waiting for them.

That is not a prediction. It is arithmetic.
