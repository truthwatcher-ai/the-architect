# Example: Column Register

**Prompt:** Write an op-ed about why the unity government is more fragile than it looks.

---

## The 112-Seat Illusion

Everybody in Putrajaya is celebrating stability. They are wrong to do so.

The unity government has survived longer than its critics predicted. That much is true. But survival and strength are not synonymous, and the political class in Malaysia has a dangerous habit of confusing the two.

Start with the arithmetic. The government commands roughly 153 seats in the 222-seat Dewan Rakyat — a comfortable margin on paper. But strip away the layers and the picture changes. Pakatan Harapan holds 82 seats. Barisan Nasional contributes 30. Gabungan Parti Sarawak provides 23. Gabungan Rakyat Sabah adds 6. The remaining seats come from smaller parties and independents whose loyalty is transactional, not ideological.

That is not a coalition. It is a consortium.

The historical parallel is instructive. In 2009, the Barisan Nasional coalition governed with a similarly impressive seat count. Within that coalition, however, UMNO's dominance masked a fundamental problem: its component parties were hollowing out. The MCA lost Chinese support. The MIC lost Indian confidence. Gerakan became irrelevant. The seats remained, but the voters behind them were leaving. By GE13, the popular vote had already flipped. By GE14, the structure collapsed entirely.

Today's unity government faces an inverted version of the same problem. Dato' Sri Anwar's coalition holds the seats, but the ideological contradictions between its members are not narrowing — they are widening. PH's reformist base grows restless with every compromise made to accommodate UMNO. BN's Malay ground feels abandoned by a party that once championed ketuanan Melayu and now sits beside DAP. Neither base is satisfied. Both are being asked to tolerate what they once defined themselves against.

And then there is Sarawak. GPS's 23 seats make Premier Abang Johari the most powerful politician most Malaysians never think about. Those 23 seats are not given freely. They come with expectations — development funding, autonomy concessions, and a veto on policies that threaten Sarawak's interests. Remove GPS from the equation and the government falls below the 130-seat mark. Remove both GPS and GRS, and the margin becomes dangerously thin.

No Prime Minister since Merdeka has governed a coalition where the second-largest bloc — the one providing the crucial margin — has no ideological commitment to the coalition's survival. GPS is not allied to Anwar's vision. GPS is allied to GPS's interests. The moment those interests are better served elsewhere, the calculation changes overnight.

The opposition understands this. Perikatan Nasional's 74 seats represent the largest single-coalition opposition bloc in Malaysian history. Mr. Muhyiddin and Dato' Seri Hadi Awang do not need to win a general election to destabilize the government. They need only to make GPS or a faction within UMNO a better offer. The anti-hopping law constrains individual MPs, but it does not prevent an entire party from withdrawing confidence in the Prime Minister.

Malaysians who remember the Sheraton Move — and they all do — know that coalition mathematics in this country can shift in a single weekend.

The unity government will likely survive until GE16. The machinery of incumbency is powerful, the opposition is divided on strategy, and the economy has provided just enough relief to prevent a crisis of confidence. But surviving until the next election is not the same as winning it. And the fractures visible today — PH's base erosion in Malay-majority seats, BN's identity crisis, GPS's transactional loyalty — will not heal with time. They will deepen.

Seats win governments. But only voters keep them.
